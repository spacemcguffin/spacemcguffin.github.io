tldr; just feeling kinda stuck

I can see why I fell out of love writing my diary back in 2012.

I began writing my diary during my latter years of high school when there’d always be a little drama happening, and I was surrounded by so many people daily. People who, like me, were still growing up, full of emotions and feelings and hormones. I used to hate the ‘drama’ but I also revelled in it at the same time. It was fun writing about it, having quite binary (and sometimes overly dramatic) opinions about it. Friends would fall out, there’d be a new relationship every week, someone would get suspended, there’d be a fight. All dumb kids’ stuff in hindsight, but it all felt exciting in amongst the chaos of raging hormones and growing pains.

Now I’m just old, and the world feels so much duller. There’s no real spark. Heightened by Covid, where we’re mostly locked down – you can’t even go to the pub for a drink with a friend. There’s no work to go to. Anxiety is skyrocketing. The internet is full of misery and woe. Everyone’s doom-scrolling. Politics, you realise, is just a bunch of people lying 24/7 and getting away with it, and nothing much changes. There’s still the ‘drama,’ but it’s all real-world stuff and it’s just knackering. Maybe I’m missing having a constant environment that I can visit for a few hours a day, before leaving it behind and going home. Like school, like university, like work.

Now I’m just in this hovel, with a fucked sleeping pattern, doing the same old shit, staring at screens all day, and rinse-repeating. I don’t even have a girlfriend or a partner, or my own place where at least I’d be able to just do my own thing 24/7 and not have to worry about other people around me so much.

I said earlier on in January that this wouldn’t be a place to vent about politics, mainly because there’s a new shedload of bullshit and crockery every day, and it’s a never-ending cycle of misery, and that’s all I’ve really consumed. I’ve watched the odd thing on TV too but beyond that… life is just at a complete standstill. I realise I’m obviously not on my own, but equally – yes, I am. Lara’s moaning that she can’t see her boyfriend, but at least she has him to lean on and talk to. Charlie’s about to have another baby. Amy has her husband and is doing a bit of baking as a business. People are all stuck in their bubbles but they all have someone or something else to keep them distracted.

I don’t really have an endpoint for this ramble. Just wanted to share a stream of consciousness and put some words to paper. Sometimes I think of topics to write about, or I have fleeting thoughts I’d like to get down, but then I just never get round to it.

Long-form writing is a dying skill, and it’s a huge shame. Diaries and journals, replaced by blogs, replaced by micro-blogs, replaced by tweets and single-line thoughts. Reading books, replaced by ebooks, replaced by audiobooks, and in some cases replaced by just not reading at all because of short attention spans. So it’s nice now and again to just set aside ten minutes to write something, even if it’s just a muddle of words with no clear through-line.

Maybe I’ll write more later. (Famous last words.)

windows

I’ve started just taking ten minutes or so to chill by my window at midnight and listen out for the church bell. I’m always up beyond midnight so I might try and do it more regularly. It’s amazing how much I feel at peace. Slight annoyance at the bell being about two minutes early, but hey. The imperfections of life.

I was thinking about the many windows I’ve looked out of at night. I miss the one at my dad’s house the most; in the tiniest boxroom with a DIY bed and a door that barely closed, the window of that room looked out across a landscape of hills, fields and country lanes. There was a streetlight directly opposite, one of those old yellowy warm ones, illuminating the stretch of main road directly below the window and reaching out into the street opposite. I miss watching lights flicker – cars driving by, homes switching lights out for the night.

I also miss the window at my second home (Moorland) which jutted out a bit. I think it’s called a dormer window. It had a deep window ledge which was big enough to sit on (back when I was a skinny teenager). Less of a view since it was a close of houses, but I kind of miss just being able to perch and actually sit on the window ledge and just be quiet for a while.

My current window looks out on to our back garden and a couple of houses opposite. Similar to the last place I lived (Clarkson), the one before that (Green Ridge), and the first (Horning). I’ve never had a truly great view, possibly in part because I’ve always had the bedroom at the back (apart from Moorland with the dormer, and my dad’s house, which are the two exceptions and the windows I miss most).

Still, I find comfort in being able to stand by my window at midnight and enjoy the silence, interrupted only by the wind or the church bell. My current window secures third place in the ranking, although the bonus of being close enough to hear the chimes almost pushes it into second.

dawn of the daleks

It still kills me that I was in central London on the very day that the production team of An Adventure in Space and Time filmed the Daleks gliding across Westminster Bridge. It was the final day of my week-long visit (in which I’d been doing some work placement stuff with Doctor Who Adventures magazine) and I woke up at about 10am in my hotel room bleary eyed and readying myself to get packed up and head back to Liverpool on the train (where I lived for university).

I woke up, opened Twitter, and there they were. Daleks, in the middle of London, from about 7am, about fifteen minutes’ walk from my hotel. The day after my birthday. My final day in London. They’d upped and left by the time I’d woken up. I’m certainly not an early morning person but, had I known about it, I’d definitely have made an attempt to get my arse out of bed just to catch a glimpse of this iconic scene being recreated. It would’ve book-ended my already fun DW-related trip beautifully. But alas. I hadn’t known prior, and to this day I’m still slightly gutted about it.

I recall it now because, by chance, I clicked on Gary Gillatt’s blog earlier and found that he’d posted an old location report he’d made on the day, originally for publication in DWM. He’d been there that morning, along with Mark Gatiss and the hoard of Daleks. It was fun to read about it again, and to also learn that, on the same day, production then packed up and headed down to BBC Television Centre to continue filming. There, they filmed the scenes of Sydney Newman pitching the show to Verity Lambert, along with Verity pestering Peter Brachacki to design the TARDIS interior. It’s quite nice to know I’d been in London on that same day, just minutes from all this Doctor Who magic, and in such close proximity to my birthday during the 50th anniversary year.

As I say, I’d been there doing some work experience alongside the team at DWA. That was a nice experience while I was there, even though there was a somewhat messy and crappy aftermath, but I’m not going to dwell on that. It soured my feelings for far too long afterwards, and very likely was part of the cause of my long-term anxiety, so I’m leaving that drama tucked away in the past. Now, I just wistfully sigh that I missed out on the chance to see a wonderful moment from 1964 being recreated after having spent the week with my head buried in pages of Doctor Who Adventures, Zygons and Sontarans.

I miss you, Doctor Who.

at the end of the diary

I read over some of Alex Day’s (nerimon) old diary earlier and realised another reason why I probably stopped writing back in 2012. Not only was I just getting on with life instead of documenting it, but my diary was largely a gossipy timeline of school friends and the drama. In hindsight, it’s amusing, but I can only assume that I saw it all as a bind and I wanted to ‘grow up’ and mature away from it, hence I deleted the whole thing. I dunno. But I also didn’t have the same kind of catalyst or reason to continue that style of diary anymore. All my close friends went their own ways, we all went into different jobs and universities and split up. School is a very closed-in environment – you see the same people every day for five years, in the same lessons, doing the same work, walking down the same corridors. There’s an ongoing narrative as things develop, day by day. Relationships develop and wither; gossip travels between friendship groups; people change but are trapped in the same environment. It’s a Big Brother house-style bubble, and my diary reflected that. There were recurring characters – Charlie, Rosie, Jess, Lara, Gabriella – and, just like a soap or a reality show, there was a constant setting in which many different character developments and plots played out, with a heavy dose of hormones thrown into the mix.

Life isn’t like that anymore though. I go to work and see the same people, sure, but we’re all adults. There’s the odd gossip, we have the odd bitch, but I’m over it and I choose not to get caught up in it if I can help it. The number of characters is also significantly less – I work in a team of three, I have acquaintances, and there are hierarchies. At school, there were hundreds of us all packed together in a powder keg, and the only oddities were the handful of teachers in the time between the socialising and the drama. And those characters were intrinsic to my life, to the point where I’d go home and sign into MSN and immediately start talking to everyone again, or I’d spend the night texting Charlie about the events of that day or gossiping about drama. The circle was so close-knit. Now, I go to work and talk to people but they all have friends far and beyond the ‘work circle’ so it’s not as relatable. We tell each other stories but that’s about it. Everyone has a much wider outreach and lives of their own. At school, there were a couple of hundred of us, and we were all each other’s friends, and very rarely would one of us be friends with someone from another school. It was a closed bubble.

I dunno if I’m explaining myself well, but I guess the short version is: my diary was never going to be the same once I left school. Probably for the best, but I do also miss having a narrative-style ongoing structure to write about. I no longer have consistent or dynamic recurring characters. The day-to-day aspect of my diary still exists but it’s very different. I don’t even see or talk to half as many people as I used to, so there’s less noise in general. I guess reading Alex’s diary was just kind of nostalgic for me because I sometimes miss that intimacy of having such a close group of friends, drama notwithstanding, and being part of little subplots that played out Monday to Friday and being able to write about it.
